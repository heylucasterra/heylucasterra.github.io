Redesign: If words are molecules, letters are atoms

March 04, 2020
São Paulo, Brazil

Finding a typeface to be my voice in the web.

No two individuals communicate in the same way. From the way you speak (words, speed, entonation, accent) to your body language, there are several things that make you you. Typefaces are the equivalent for any written medium.

Here's a practical example: Valter Hugo Mãe is one of my favourite contemporary authors. I usually buy his books from the same Biblioteca Azul collection. The typeface Silva made by Brazilian type foundry Black Letra morphed into his voice (or at least the idea of it) as I read his books.

Picking fonts and testing is a very difficult process. Combining different fonts and make them match baseline is even tougher and time consuming. For now, I'll be happy to pick one font only that works for both titles and body. After some digging I got to 5 sans-serif finalists that point in two different directions.

Direction A

This group of typefaces have expression while still extremely simple. I want my typeface of choice to feel humble, yet with personality. But not distracting. Let's take a look at them.

National 2 by Klim (left), Theinhardt by Optimo (center) and NB National Std by Neubau (right).

In the example above, they are all set at equal sizes (as I write this I also realized two of them also share the same name). Theinhardt feels too dull when placed among the other two. I appreciate how Klim's National 2 seem to have received a lot of love and thought in it's project, specially in the terminations where you can clearly see the changes in width, with a bit of calligraphic influence. Neubau's NB National on the other hand has the most obvious geometry. Look at that perfect small-caps a...

Let's see how they render in a paragraph.

Klim's National 2 (top), Optimo's Theinhardt (mid), Neubau's NB National (bottom)

A lot has changed. Theinhardt suddenly doesn't feel as dull. It's actually the easiest to read, even if you zoom out or try it in smaller sizes. I think most people would agree that National 2 on the top is real delight and NB National might work for titles but for the body it simply sets an awkward rythm.

Direction B

I know, after a month looking at typefaces I have Graphik in this list. I know it's been overused but the main reason I'm bringing it here is that I found another font, called Abitare and discarded it because Graphik seems better solved. And then I found Regola, which is an imperfect typeface but something in it makes it very attractive.

Graphik by Commercial Type (left) and Regola Pro by Think Work Observe (right).

At the first look, these two might look pretty much the same. Give it another look. I have been observing how they solved different letters and also tried them in different explorations (might share my experiments some other time) and it's been illuminating to see the differences between these two.

In the example above, Regola is visibly wider than Graphik and set at the same weight it also seems slightly thicker. The small-caps a has more white space to breathe and you'll notice there's something in the corners of the Z that make Graphik seem more fragile.
